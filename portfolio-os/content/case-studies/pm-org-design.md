## Framing

A VP Product inheriting a team needs to know two things: can this 
person grow PMs deliberately, and can they hold a performance standard 
without losing the team. This story is about building the system that 
did both  -  from scratch, inside a company that had never needed one.

## Context

BCT operated with a startup mindset inside a large enterprise. Titles 
were informal, expectations were implicit, and advancement was assumed rather 
than structured. That worked until it did not.

The signal was specific: PMs started coming to me asking for promotions 
I did not think they had earned. Not because they were wrong to ask  -  
because there was no shared language for what earning a promotion 
actually meant. I was holding a standard in my head that nobody else 
could see. That is not a performance problem. That is a system problem.

## Decision

I could have handled promotion requests case by case  -  the faster, 
lower-effort path. Instead I decided to build a formal career ladder 
that made expectations visible to everyone on the team simultaneously.

The reasoning: implicit standards protect the manager, not the team. 
If only I know what good looks like at each level, I am the bottleneck 
and the arbiter. A transparent ladder shifts that  -  PMs can self-assess, 
peers can calibrate against each other, and I can give feedback that 
references a shared framework rather than my personal judgment.

## Action

Built a five-dimension career ladder covering Insight, Strategy, 
Execution, Outcomes, and Leadership  -  with explicit level descriptors 
from Associate PM through Principal PM. Every PM was rated annually 
across all five dimensions.

The implementation problem emerged immediately: variance between 
self-ratings and my ratings was large. A PM would rate themselves 9 
out of 10 on a dimension I would rate 3. That gap, if it surfaces 
only at review time, destroys trust and feels like ambush.

The fix was a dedicated monthly 1-1 specifically on the career ladder. 
Not project work, not roadmap  -  exclusively development feedback. 
Every month I gave specific, referenced feedback on what was not 
working against the ladder criteria. By the time annual reviews came, 
there were zero surprises. The rating was already known because we 
had been building toward it together for twelve months.

The ownership model ran alongside the ladder: S and M features fully 
in the PM's hands with no approval needed. L, XL, and XXL required 
a written Confluence brief covering customer impact, strategic fit, 
solution uniqueness, and emotional delight  -  and my sign-off. 
This gave junior PMs real authority from day one while creating a 
forcing function for structured thinking on high-stakes decisions. 
Early briefs came back for rework. I held the standard deliberately. 
By the 12-month mark, submission quality had improved measurably.

## Outcome

Three PMs promoted to Senior PM through the ladder system. The 
promotion I am most proud of: a senior designer who transitioned 
into product and eventually became a Senior PM. They had no 
traditional PM background. What they had was the ability to take 
feedback seriously and work on it visibly. The ladder gave them 
a map. The monthly 1-1s gave them honest navigation. The development 
was evident to everyone on the team, not just to me.

The ownership model changed the culture of how PMs thought about 
decisions. They stopped asking for permission on small things and 
started writing better briefs on big ones. That shift  -  from 
approval-seeking to structured autonomy  -  is what a functioning 
PM org looks like.

## What I almost got wrong

I almost resolved the variance problem by softening my ratings to 
close the gap. When someone believes they are a 9 and you tell them 
they are a 3, the socially easier move is to say 6 and avoid the 
conversation. I did not do that  -  but I felt the pull toward it.

The monthly 1-1 cadence was my solution to that pressure. If I am 
giving someone a 3 at annual review, they should have heard the 
specific reasons for that rating eleven times before the number 
appears on paper. That makes honesty sustainable. Without the 
cadence, holding the standard would have eventually cost me 
relationships. With it, the standard became something we developed 
toward together.
