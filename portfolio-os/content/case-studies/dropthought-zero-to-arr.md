## Framing

This is a story about knowing when your product is wrong and moving 
fast enough to fix it before the market moves on. The signal was not 
data  -  it was an executive telling me directly the product looked like 
a Mickey Mouse tool. Four months later we signed Cisco.

## Context

Dropthought was designed for SMB. The product reflected that  -  lightweight, 
self-serve, optimised for ease of setup over depth of capability. When we 
took it upmarket the buyer signal was customer silence. Not complaints. 
Not churn. Silence. Wrong buyer, wrong use case, wrong positioning. 
We were getting meetings but nothing was converting.

The moment that clarified it: an enterprise executive looked at the 
product demo and said it looked like a Mickey Mouse product. That 
is not a positioning problem. That is a product problem.

## Decision

We had two options. Reposition the SMB product with better enterprise 
messaging  -  the cheaper, faster choice. Or rebuild for enterprise from 
the ground up  -  the harder choice that would take four months and 
carry real execution risk.

I chose the rebuild. The reasoning: messaging cannot fix a product that 
enterprise buyers can see is not designed for them. You can dress it up 
but the judgment call a sophisticated buyer makes in the first ten 
minutes of a demo is based on product depth, not copy.

Before rebuilding I ran structured validation research to make sure 
we were rebuilding toward the right target: ethnographic studies, 
JTBD research, user motivation and pain point mapping against specific 
enterprise user types. The goal was to not rebuild blind.

That research surfaced Executive Briefing Centers in Fortune 100 
companies as the beachhead. Large event volume, underserved by 
existing tools, and structurally positioned for account expansion  -  
a win in EBC could open the rest of the enterprise.

## Action

Four-month rebuild targeted specifically at the EBC use case. 
Every product decision in that rebuild was tested against the 
user types the research had identified  -  not against what we 
thought enterprise wanted, against what the research showed 
they actually needed.

Cisco was the target. One of the largest EBC operations in 
the world. If the redesigned product worked there it would work 
anywhere in the segment.

## Outcome

We won Cisco. The solution worked well enough that five more 
hi-tech companies signed on for the same use case without 
requiring us to reposition. That replication is the validation 
signal  -  when you stop explaining and customers start referring, 
you have found the market.

That beachhead became the foundation of the path to $6M ARR.

## What I almost got wrong

I almost tried to fix it with messaging before accepting it was 
a product problem. The Mickey Mouse comment was the forcing 
function. Without that direct feedback I might have spent another 
quarter repositioning a product that needed a complete redesign. 
The lesson: customer silence is a signal but it is an ambiguous one. 
Sometimes you need someone to say the hard thing directly before 
you move fast enough.
