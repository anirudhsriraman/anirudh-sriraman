# PM Org Design: Real Ownership from Day One

The most common failure mode in PM teams is ambiguity about who actually owns what. Senior PMs hoard decisions. Junior PMs wait for permission. Nothing ships on time, and nobody grows.

I built two tools to fix this: a career ladder that defines what progression actually looks like, and an ownership model that ties authority to feature complexity rather than seniority.

## The 5-Dimension Career Ladder

Progress on all five dimensions defines progression from Associate PM through Principal PM. No single dimension is enough on its own.

**1. Insight** — Can they identify the right problem? Do they distinguish symptoms from causes?

**2. Strategy** — Can they make trade-off decisions? Do they think in terms of bets, not features?

**3. Execution** — Can they ship? Do they manage dependencies, resolve blockers, and hold a release date?

**4. Outcomes** — Do they own the metric after launch? Do they close the loop between what shipped and what changed?

**5. Leadership** — Do they make the people around them better? Can they run a room they don't formally own?

## The Ownership Model

Authority is tied to feature size — not job title.

| Feature size | What it means | PM authority |
|---|---|---|
| **S / M** | Small or medium scope, limited eng effort | Full PM authority — no approval needed |
| **L** | Large scope, 2+ sprint cycles | Written brief required before scoping |
| **XL / XXL** | Multi-quarter, cross-functional | Brief + stakeholder alignment required |

The brief requirement for large features is not a bureaucratic gate — it is a forcing function. Writing the brief surfaces assumptions, aligns engineering early, and prevents the most common source of mid-sprint scope creep.

> "This gave junior PMs real authority from day one — and gave senior PMs visibility into the work without micromanaging it."

## The outcome

Three PMs were promoted during a 15-month stretch that included a VP of Engineering departure and a $2M delivery under pressure. The ownership model meant the team kept shipping without waiting for leadership to stabilise.
