The decision: Arabic NLP was a genuine competitive differentiator for GCC enterprise — and a genuine technical challenge. Arabic has significant dialectal variation and limited training data compared to English. We chose to build a custom model rather than rely on off-the-shelf solutions, because the use case — enterprise CX sentiment analysis in regulated GCC markets — required accuracy and explainability that generic models couldn't guarantee.

The problem we had to solve: A custom model is only as good as the data it's trained on. Our initial classification accuracy was 52% — too low to put in front of enterprise clients. The bottleneck was labeling quality: without consistent, high-quality human annotations, the model couldn't improve. We needed a way to produce training data at scale with measurable quality controls.

What I built: HumInt — an in-house human annotation pipeline with automated sentence parsing, dual-annotator consensus workflows, and IRR-based quality gates. Every labeled data point required agreement between two annotators before entering the training set. Disagreements triggered a structured adjudication process. An annotator productivity dashboard tracked throughput, agreement rates, and escalation patterns — reducing labeling cycle time by 35%.

The result: NLP classification accuracy improved from 52% to 78%. The HumInt pipeline processed 5.4M+ labeled data points at 90% inter-annotator agreement over three years. Arabic-language sentiment analysis became a genuine competitive differentiator in GCC markets — something clients in UAE, Oman, and Singapore couldn't get from any competitor without equivalent investment.

03 — On Building AI

Four lessons. Two frameworks.

I've built AI that worked and watched AI fail — sometimes my own. The failures taught me more. Most enterprise AI fails because the organisation wasn't ready to absorb it.

Lesson 01 · Adoption
The demo is not the product
At a large regional bank, our AI Q&A feature landed brilliantly in the executive demo. Usage dropped to near zero after week two. Executive demos optimise for wow. Daily workflows optimise for reliability and fit with existing routines. I redesigned every subsequent deployment around saved query templates, weekly reporting cadences, and insight ownership mapping — not the demo moment.
Lesson 02 · Trust
Black-box AI gets overridden. Always.
CX teams couldn't trust AI-generated insights because the system couldn't explain its reasoning in terms that mapped to their KPIs. They went back to Excel. Explainability isn't a nice-to-have — it's the condition for adoption. I now build human-in-the-loop workflows into AI feature specs before a line of code is written.
Lesson 03 · Readiness
AI readiness is organisational, not technical
We brought a Level 5 AI solution to a Level 2 organisation. Their data discipline and change management bandwidth couldn't absorb it — not because the product was wrong, but because the sequencing was. I now run a CX maturity assessment before scoping any AI features. The question isn't "can the technology do this" — it's "can this team act on what it surfaces."
Lesson 04 · Governance
Ship fast, govern early
We built a custom Arabic NLP model — and the governance layer was what made it trustworthy. HumInt, our in-house annotation pipeline, enforced dual-annotator consensus and IRR-based quality gates on every data point before it entered training. The model was only as good as the data — and the data was only as good as the process governing it. I apply the same logic to all AI features today: move fast on the capability, but own the governance layer — accuracy thresholds, hallucination rate limits, human review workflows — before GA launch, not after a client complaint.
Frameworks I built
Tool 01 · Pre-Deployment
AI-CX Client Readiness Diagnostic
▾

A 5-dimension discovery framework that determines which AI product tier a GCC enterprise client is ready to deploy — before a contract is signed. Built from 40+ enterprise deployments across BFSI, GovTech, and Telecom.

What the tiers mean
Tier A Feedback + Analytics — Collect feedback, run NLP, view dashboards. Good for organisations just starting to centralise CX data.
Tier B + Workflow Automation — Feedback triggers automated recovery actions. A detractor score fires a DTWorks ticket. Requires defined processes to automate.
Tier C + Natural Language Q&A — Analysts ask questions of the feedback corpus in plain English. Only valuable if the team has the data literacy to interrogate it.
Tier D + Agentic AI — AI acts autonomously: routes, responds, escalates without human initiation. Requires organisational trust, leadership accountability, and mature data governance.
5 dimensions scored Low / Medium / High
DataFeedback centralised, Arabic handled, real-time accessible — not just collected
TechnologyIntegration capability, DESC ISR / PDPL compliance, cloud vs. on-prem constraints
ProcessClosed-loop mechanism exists, cross-functional handoffs defined and followed
PeopleCX team can self-serve data, has authority to act on insights — not just view them
LeadershipNamed executive sponsor, CX tied to revenue KPIs — not just tracked and reported
Critical gates — a single failure caps the tier
Tier BProcess must be Medium — you cannot automate a process that hasn't been defined
Tier CPeople must be High — natural language Q&A becomes shelf-ware if the team lacks the literacy to interrogate it
Tier DLeadership must be High — autonomous AI without an accountable executive sponsor is a governance failure waiting to happen

The most important rule: score what the client demonstrates, not what they claim. Every client believes they are more ready than they are. A wrong tier recommendation at the sale becomes a failed implementation six months later.

Three tracks — change management, frameworks I've built, and how I use analytics to make decisions.

Waterfall → Agile
Changing how teams ship
I led the transition from waterfall to sprint-based Agile — introducing two-track sprint planning, staggered engineering cycles, and structured backlog governance with explicit go/no-go gates. 50% improvement in development velocity. The structural shift also changed culture: PMs stopped taking requirements and started defending prioritisation decisions with data.
Design Thinking
Embedding evidence into design decisions
Restructured the team into journey-aligned pods. Established a quarterly persona cadence where PMs presented updated client insights to designers, engineers, and presales. Over time the internal language shifted: teams stopped arguing about what they believed users wanted and started asking what the evidence showed. That shift is the clearest sign design thinking had become embedded, not performative.
AI Adoption
Automating the work nobody wanted to do
Introduced AI tooling to automate three high-friction tasks: user research analysis, FAQ documentation updates, and sprint release notes. These were unglamorous workflow improvements that freed the team to focus on decisions only humans could make. Also used AI-assisted QA during a critical delivery — recovered three weeks of timeline without adding headcount.
Culture
Making CX everyone's accountability
When I audited our NPS program, CS was selectively excluding accounts they believed would score poorly — protecting their response rate OKR at the cost of program integrity. Established universal distribution as non-negotiable. Decoupled response rate from CS performance metrics. Retention, NPS, ease-of-use scores, and the BAU metric embedded as shared OKRs across CS, product, and engineering.
DEAR Framework
Predicting churn 6 months before it happens
Built the DEAR framework — Deployment, Engagement, Adoption, ROI — as the leading indicator model for client health. Weights derived from correlating scores against actual renewal outcomes. Clients with healthy deployment scores 6 months before renewal had 15% higher gross renewal rates. CS analysts had a commercially grounded reason to act early — not wait for the renewal conversation.
CS Analyst Framework · X/O Model
Six ways to turn CX data into decisions
I built a structured framework for CS analysts to move from raw feedback to action — based on six analysis patterns:

X Why — find something in operational data (O-data) and look for an explanation in experience data (X-data). Why did CSAT drop this quarter? Look at what customers said.

O Drivers — find something in X-data and identify the operational situation causing it. High effort scores — is it a process failure, a system failure, or a staffing issue?

X&O Predict — build projections based on combined X and O data. Churn likelihood scores built from both NPS trends and usage behaviour.

X&O Personalise — adjust how you treat customers based on a combination of X and O signals. A VIP detractor with declining usage gets a different recovery action than a new account with a low score.

X&O Alert — send proactive alerts based on combined signals. When NPS drops and usage falls simultaneously, fire a CS intervention — don't wait for the renewal conversation.

X Value — measure the value of improving experiences by examining the impact on business results. A 5-point NPS improvement in the onboarding journey — what did it do to 90-day retention?

The framework gave every analyst a shared vocabulary — instead of "here's some feedback," the output became "here's an O Driver finding, here's the X-data that explains it, and here's the X&O Alert we should fire before this account churns."
BAU North Star
Defining what good actually looks like
Personally defined Dropthought's North Star: a Best Active User identifies and shares an insight consumed by 5+ people in 30 days, leading to 3+ documented improvements in a quarter. Chose this because it captured the full value chain — not just usage, but insight generation, sharing, and action. Gave product and CS a common language for client health at the user level, not just the account level.
Three-Layer Metrics Architecture
Connecting CX signals to commercial outcomes
Designed a three-layer architecture: signal quality (response rate, volume) → experience quality (NPS, CSAT, verbatim themes) → business outcomes (GRR, NRR, expansion revenue). Built Tableau reporting connecting all three layers into a single leadership view. CX programs lose organisational credibility when metrics are reported in isolation. Making the connection to renewal rate turned CX metrics from a reporting function into a strategic investment input.
Mixpanel · Engagement Funnel Design
Defining what an engaged user actually looks like
Before implementing Mixpanel I had to answer a harder question: what does product engagement actually mean for a CX analytics platform? I defined a four-stage funnel — first-time, new, ordinary, and engaged — with specific behavioural criteria for each transition. An engaged user logged in every weekday, spent 30+ minutes weekly reviewing results, and responded to at least 5% of feedback received. Without this definition, Mixpanel would have measured activity. With it, it measured value delivery.
Totango · Health Scoring
Predicting renewal at the user level
Built an eight-dimension client health score in Totango with weights derived from correlating scores against actual renewal outcomes. Correlated all three survey touchpoints against Totango scores at the individual user level — identifying users who gave high NPS but showed declining engagement. That pattern predicted silent churn that survey data alone would never surface. Retention moved from 70% to 96%.
Tableau · Executive Reporting
One view that connected CX to revenue
Built Tableau reporting that connected all three metric layers — signal, experience, and business outcomes — into a single leadership dashboard presented directly to clients as a strategic advisory tool. The goal: a CFO should be able to look at one screen and understand how NPS movement connects to renewal rate. Institutionalised across the CS insights team as a repeatable methodology — not a one-off executive presentation.
Data Integrity
Fixing the measurement before trusting the numbers
Analytics is only as good as the data feeding it. When I audited Dropthought's NPS program I found CS was selectively excluding accounts likely to score poorly — contaminating the baseline. Fixed the distribution methodology first, established IRR-based quality gates on the NLP annotation pipeline, and built reconciliation checks between CRM data and survey responses. The principle: measure what's real, not what looks good. Every framework I built assumes the data has been cleaned first.
Growing PMs · Career Ladder
A structured system for PM growth — not just promotions
I built a five-dimension career ladder for the product team covering Insight (customer focus, analytics, competitive intelligence), Strategy (roadmap rationale, alignment), Execution (problem articulation, shipping cadence), Outcomes (customer and business impact), and Leadership behaviours (communication, collaboration, decisiveness). Every PM was rated annually across all dimensions with explicit level descriptors — Associate PM through Principal PM. The ladder made growth visible and removed ambiguity about what "good" looked like at each level. All three PMs who were rated and developed against it were promoted to Senior PM.
Growing PMs · Ownership Model
Trust with structure — not trust without guardrails
I built a tiered ownership model: S and M features were fully in the PM's hands with no approval needed. L, XL, and XXL required a written Confluence brief covering customer impact, strategic fit, solution uniqueness, and emotional delight for the user — and my sign-off. The threshold was engineering effort, not seniority. This gave junior PMs real authority from day one while creating a forcing function for structured thinking on high-stakes decisions. Early submissions came back for rework. I held the standard deliberately. Over time the quality of submissions improved measurably — not because I lowered the bar, but because the team learned what a good answer actually looked like.
