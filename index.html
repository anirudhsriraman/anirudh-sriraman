From Mickey Mouse to Cisco: Validating an Enterprise Product

This is a story about knowing when your product is wrong and moving fast enough to fix it before the market moves on. The signal was not data - it was an executive telling me directly the product looked like a Mickey Mouse tool. Four months later we signed Cisco.

Context

Dropthought was designed for SMB. The product reflected that - lightweight, self-serve, optimised for ease of setup over depth of capability. When we took it upmarket the buyer signal was customer silence. Not complaints. Not churn. Silence. Wrong buyer, wrong use case, wrong positioning. We were getting meetings but nothing was converting.

The moment that clarified it: an enterprise executive looked at the product demo and said it looked like a Mickey Mouse product. That is not a positioning problem. That is a product problem.

Decision

We had two options. Reposition the SMB product with better enterprise messaging - the cheaper, faster choice. Or rebuild for enterprise from the ground up - the harder choice that would take four months and carry real execution risk.

I chose the rebuild. The reasoning: messaging cannot fix a product that enterprise buyers can see is not designed for them. You can dress it up but the judgment call a sophisticated buyer makes in the first ten minutes of a demo is based on product depth, not copy.

Before rebuilding I ran structured validation research to make sure we were rebuilding toward the right target: ethnographic studies, JTBD research, user motivation and pain point mapping against specific enterprise user types. The goal was to not rebuild blind.

That research surfaced Executive Briefing Centers in Fortune 100 companies as the beachhead. Large event volume, underserved by existing tools, and structurally positioned for account expansion - a win in EBC could open the rest of the enterprise.

Action

Four-month rebuild targeted specifically at the EBC use case. Every product decision in that rebuild was tested against the user types the research had identified - not against what we thought enterprise wanted, against what the research showed they actually needed.

Cisco was the target. One of the largest EBC operations in the world. If the redesigned product worked there it would work anywhere in the segment.

Outcome

We won Cisco. The solution worked well enough that five more hi-tech companies signed on for the same use case without requiring us to reposition. That replication is the validation signal - when you stop explaining and customers start referring, you have found the market.

That beachhead became the foundation of the path to $6M ARR.

What I almost got wrong

I almost tried to fix it with messaging before accepting it was a product problem. The Mickey Mouse comment was the forcing function. Without that direct feedback I might have spent another quarter repositioning a product that needed a complete redesign. The lesson: customer silence is a signal but it is an ambiguous one. Sometimes you need someone to say the hard thing directly before you move fast enough.

Building the PM Growth System: From Startup Chaos to

A VP Product inheriting a team needs to know two things: can this person grow PMs deliberately, and can they hold a performance standard without losing the team. This story is about building the system that did both - from scratch, inside a company that had never needed one.

Context

BCT operated with a startup mindset inside a large enterprise. Titles were informal, expectations were implicit, and advancement was assumed rather than structured. That worked until it did not.

The signal was specific: PMs started coming to me asking for promotions I did not think they had earned. Not because they were wrong to ask - because there was no shared language for what earning a promotion actually meant. I was holding a standard in my head that nobody else could see. That is not a performance problem. That is a system problem.

Decision

I could have handled promotion requests case by case - the faster, lower-effort path. Instead I decided to build a formal career ladder that made expectations visible to everyone on the team simultaneously.

The reasoning: implicit standards protect the manager, not the team. If only I know what good looks like at each level, I am the bottleneck and the arbiter. A transparent ladder shifts that - PMs can self-assess, peers can calibrate against each other, and I can give feedback that references a shared framework rather than my personal judgment.

Action

Built a five-dimension career ladder covering Insight, Strategy, Execution, Outcomes, and Leadership - with explicit level descriptors from Associate PM through Principal PM. Every PM was rated annually across all five dimensions.

The implementation problem emerged immediately: variance between self-ratings and my ratings was large. A PM would rate themselves 9 out of 10 on a dimension I would rate 3. That gap, if it surfaces only at review time, destroys trust and feels like ambush.

The fix was a dedicated monthly 1-1 specifically on the career ladder. Not project work, not roadmap - exclusively development feedback. Every month I gave specific, referenced feedback on what was not working against the ladder criteria. By the time annual reviews came, there were zero surprises. The rating was already known because we had been building toward it together for twelve months.

The ownership model ran alongside the ladder: S and M features fully in the PM's hands with no approval needed. L, XL, and XXL required a written Confluence brief covering customer impact, strategic fit, solution uniqueness, and emotional delight - and my sign-off. This gave junior PMs real authority from day one while creating a forcing function for structured thinking on high-stakes decisions. Early briefs came back for rework. I held the standard deliberately. By the 12-month mark, submission quality had improved measurably.

Outcome

Three PMs promoted to Senior PM through the ladder system. The promotion I am most proud of: a senior designer who transitioned into product and eventually became a Senior PM. They had no traditional PM background. What they had was the ability to take feedback seriously and work on it visibly. The ladder gave them a map. The monthly 1-1s gave them honest navigation. The development was evident to everyone on the team, not just to me.

The ownership model changed the culture of how PMs thought about decisions. They stopped asking for permission on small things and started writing better briefs on big ones. That shift - from approval-seeking to structured autonomy - is what a functioning PM org looks like.

What I almost got wrong

I almost resolved the variance problem by softening my ratings to close the gap. When someone believes they are a 9 and you tell them they are a 3, the socially easier move is to say 6 and avoid the conversation. I did not do that - but I felt the pull toward it.

The monthly 1-1 cadence was my solution to that pressure. If I am giving someone a 3 at annual review, they should have heard the specific reasons for that rating eleven times before the number appears on paper. That makes honesty sustainable. Without the cadence, holding the standard would have eventually cost me relationships. With it, the standard became something we developed toward together.

Government & Telecom CX at Scale

Government & Telecom CX at Scale **Metrics:** 810K+ annual calls | 25% CSAT improvement | 30% faster service delivery

Context

Two enterprise clients in regulated GCC markets needed to modernise their citizen and customer contact operations. TD123 was a Brunei government mandate to centralise national citizen services through a single contact centre, handling 810K+ calls annually with no existing omnichannel infrastructure. Imagine Telecom was a regional carrier with declining CSAT scores and slow service delivery cycles, losing ground to competitors on both resolution speed and upsell conversion.

Decision

Rather than adapt a generic contact centre product, I led the decision to build tailored platform configurations for each client: a high-availability omnichannel layer for TD123 handling 810K+ annual calls, optimised for regulated-market compliance, and a workflow-driven service delivery model for Imagine Telecom focused on resolution speed and upsell trigger points. Both deployments shared a common feedback integration layer to enable continuous CSAT measurement without separate tooling overhead.

Action

For TD123, led product development across platform architecture, workflow engine design, and omnichannel model configuration. Worked directly with Brunei government stakeholders to translate regulatory and operational requirements into a contact centre spec that achieved 95% of calls answered within 10 seconds and an 80%+ first call resolution rate. For Imagine Telecom, redesigned service delivery workflows to eliminate handoff delays and embedded upsell prompts at natural resolution points. Aligned both clients to a shared feedback integration spec to enable CSAT tracking at scale.

Outcome

TD123 handled 810K+ annual calls with a 4.1/5 CSAT score, 95% answer rate within 10 seconds, and 80%+ first call resolution. Imagine Telecom saw a 25% improvement in CSAT, a 30% reduction in service delivery times, and a 20% increase in upsell success rate. Both deployments demonstrated that a single platform architecture, configured for context, could meet government-grade reliability requirements and commercial CX targets simultaneously.
